Introduction

The rule B2n3-q5y6c/S23-k, also known as VesselLife, is similar to Life, but has some distinctive differences. All four of Life’s basic spaceships work. Its most distinguishing feature is that patterns like boats, barges, and ships of various lengths are extremely common, and these can act as tag-alongs behind a glider, allowing extrusion of ships of arbitrary length. Another distinctive characteristic is the B2n condition, which allows patterns to interact at greater distance, and makes many synthesis techniques difficult or impossible. This rule is less explosive than Life, and there are no known patterns with infinite chaotic growth; most small patterns become stable after at most a few hundred generations.

Although not generally considered a still-life, the empty field (i.e. vacuum) technically fulfills most the criteria, and it occurs sufficiently frequently to warrant being mentioned. For example, it is the most common result of two gliders colliding. (Since it has no living cells, it fails to be an actual object, in much the same way that 1 is not considered a prime number, even though it has no other prime factors.)
