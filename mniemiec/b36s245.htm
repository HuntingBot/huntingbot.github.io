Introduction

The rule B36/S245, also known as the Logarithmic Replicator rule, behaves very differently from Life. Its most distinguishing feature is that it has a small common replicator, the shuttle. The shuttle forms the basis for most complex constructions in this rule. Every 102 generations, the shuttle flips over and drops an egg, similar to how Life's queen bee shuttle works. However, unlike the queen bee, which is destroyed if it runs into its own egg, the shuttle splits into two asynchronous shuttles.

The interactions between the shuttles and their eggs and other junk they leave behind is much more complicated than that of the replicator in HighLife, but because this rule is even less explosive than Life is, the shuttle's activity tends to remain fairly close to its axis of growth.

The shuttle itself is extremely versatile:

  • By itself, it grows irregularly, expanding in both directions, filling the space between the ends with unpredictable junk, and eventually stabilizing.
  • Two shuttles can be combined to form a clean replicator that behaves like a one-dimensional XOR cellular automaton embedded in a two-dimensional space, and whose Feynman diagram is a Sierpinski gasket. This is similar to the replicator in HighLife.
  • If one end is capped by an oscillator that eats eggs, this forms a single-ended wick-stretcher. If both ends are capped, it forms an oscillator of arbitrarily high period.
  • Multiple shuttles in tandem can also be used to create oscillators, clean spaceships, puffers, or guns.

Synthesizing pseudo-objects is generally fairly easy, either by simultaneously shooting two edgewise objects at each other, or by first synthesizing one, and then adding the other (or adding another smaller object and then growing it into the desired object.

  • These small objects can be added to existing objects: P, beehive (and turned into pond), cis carrier (and turned to trans carrier), medium blinker, heavy blinker, toad, and trans egg.
  • These small objects cannot yet be added, but can be synthesized edgewise: W (and turned into Z, P, crab, and long Z).
  • These objects cannot yet be added, so pseudo-objects involving them cannot yet be synthesized, unless all other pieces can be added: X, pinwheel, R, loaf, shuriken, shuriken w/domino, cis egg, cam, and table on table. (Groups of 2…4 Xs and Rs can be synthesized simultaneously).

Soups in this rule tend to be quite tame, with one exception - if they form a shuttle. The shuttle is a natural replicator. It changes direction every 102 generations after dropping an egg. However, if an egg is in its path, it creates large sparks, and then reforms two shuttles after 96 generations, so its population forms an irregularly-growing sawtooth. There are many ways of combining shuttles to create spaceships, puffers, and guns. Most of the three-glider conversion tools involve two gliders forming a shuttle, and a third glider hitting it before it grows too large. Many of the larger exotic still-lifes with even symmetry evolve from the shuttle.

David Eppstein wrote an article about replicators in this rule.

Two shuttles can combine to form a regular replicator.

The point on the W can eat gliders. Several other oscillators that have similar protruding points (e.g. pinwheel and R) can also eat gliders. One phase of the glider has a protruding point that can eat several objects.

A certain group of adjacent P4 oscillators can interact with each other, in a manner isomorphic to a one-dimensional cellular automaton with a 3-cell neighborhood, four living states (X, right-facing R, left-facing R, and table), and bounded at both ends by vacuum. Every four generations in B36/S245 is equivalent to one generation in the one-dimensional cellular automaton. This produce oscillators of several different periods that are multiples of four¹. Computer searches of all patterns containing up to 14 such adjacent 3×3 cells have discovered oscillators of periods 2¹, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 28, 32, 36, 40, 44, 48, 52, 56, 64, 68, 72, 76, 84, 88, 92, 96, 108, 112, 132, 136, 140, 156, 164, 168, 180, 212, 224, 276, 284, 304, 324, 356, 484, 504, 832, 872, 1628, 2424, and also many pseudo-oscillators consisting of combinations of smaller oscillators in this series.

(Note¹: 2 is not a multiple of 4; the period 2 X is included here because it is a member of this series.) There are many oscillators of most of these periods. For brevity, only one example is shown for each period.

1 methuselah

This rule's most distinguishing feature is that it has a small common replicator, the shuttle. The shuttle forms the basis for most complex constructions in this rule. Every 102 generations, the shuttle flips over and drops an egg, similar to how Life's queen bee shuttle works. However, unlike the queen bee, which is destroyed if it runs into its own egg, the shuttle splits into two asynchronous shuttles.

The interactions between the shuttles and their eggs and other junk they leave behind is much more complicated than that of the replicator in HighLife, but because this rule is even less explosive than Life is, the shuttle's activity tends to remain fairly close to its axis of growth.

The shuttle itself is extremely versatile:

  • By itself, it grows irregularly, expanding in both directions, filling the space between the ends with unpredictable junk, and eventually stabilizing.
  • Two shuttles can be combined to form a clean replicator that behaves like a one-dimensional XOR cellular automaton embedded in a two-dimensional space, and whose Feynman diagram is a Sierpinski gasket. This is similar to the replicator in HighLife.
  • If one end is capped by an oscillator that eats eggs, this forms a single-ended wick-stretcher. If both ends are capped, it forms an oscillator of arbitrarily high period.
  • Multiple shuttles in tandem can also be used to create oscillators, clean spaceships, puffers, or guns.
