Introduction

The rule B36/S23, more commonly known as HighLife, is almost identical to normal Life. It differs only in that birth also occurs when a cell has 6 living neighbors. This results in behavior that is very similar to Life, but with a few differences:

  • The list of still-lifes is a strict subset of the still-lifes in Life. Still-lifes containing any of four configurations are no longer viable. These includes ships, buns (or domino behind eater tail, which uses the same neighborhood), loaves with bit, and pairs of inducting lines of three (e.g. dead spark coil, docks, etc.). There are no additional still-lifes. Also, oscillators whose stators include any of these components also fail; in some cases, stator variants can be constructed that don't include these components.
  • Most period 2 oscillators and several higher-period oscillators continue to work; however, the higher the period, the less likely this is to be true. There are several new oscillators.
  • The basic spaceships (glider, LWSS, MWSS, and HWSS) still work, as do a small number of others. There are several new spaceships.
  • Most of life's common patterns (e.g. traffic light, honeyfarm, fleet, bakery, B heptomino, Pi heptomino, R pentomino, etc.) evolve very differently, so oscillators based on them also fail (e.g. pulsar, and pre-pulsar shuttles).
  • The queen bee shuttle no longer works, but there is an equally useful common period 24 shuttle that can be used to construct glider guns.
  • There is a very small common replicator that is also extremely versatile. Every 12 generations, a living replicator unit splits into two adjacent ones, while two units attempting to occupy the same space cancel each other out. This behaves like a one-dimensional XOR cellular automaton embedded in a two-dimensional space. Its Feynman diagram is a Sierpinski gasket.
    • By itself, it is a double-ended wick-stretcher with a sawtooth population growth.
    • If one end is capped (e.g. by a block or eater, or another replicator along a line of symmetry), this forms a single-ended wick-stretcher.
    • If both ends are capped, it forms an oscillator of arbitrarily high period. Examples of periods 48, 72, and 96, are shown here, and all can be extended by arbitrarily large multiples of 48.
    • It can pull a blinker, resulting in the Bomber spaceship.
    • If the pull reaction is slowed by moving the replicator farther away from the blinker, this results in a wick stretcher that extrudes a growing spaceship whose head advances faster than its tail recedes.
    • A complicated chain of replicators can displace a blinker, and two such reactions can push a blinker diagonally. This allows construction of arbitrarily slow wick stretchers.
    • Combining the blinker push and blinker pull reactions allows creation of the arbitrarily slow Basilisk spaceships.
    • Out-of-phase replicators can act as fence posts for each other, allowing construction of oscillators of periods that are not a multiple of 24. (A period 59 example has been constructed, but is too large to show here.)

The lists of still-lifes, pseudo-still-lifes, quasi-still-lifes, and constellations only show small and easily synthesized ones. The lists of oscillators and pseudo-oscillators show all basic ones from Life that also work in HighLife, as well as new ones that only work in HighLife (marked with ¹). but for brevity, only the simplest stator variants, and omit larger variants. For the same reason, lists of pseudo-oscillators and pseudo-spaceships are omitted, as pseudo-objects are just trivial combinations of smaller objects. Due to the fact that most Life period 2 oscillators also work in HighLife, only a few small examples are shown.

Some Life oscillators do not work in HighLife because their stators become unstable; these oscillators, with stators adjusted to work in both Life and HighLife, are marked with ²

1 c/6 diagonal spaceship

– ⇧ ↑ ↓ ⇩ PNG RLE APG LIS SOF ZIP SRCH

Bomber; Glider 12056; 19P48H8V8.1¹ [4]

The Bomber is based on a mechanism where a replicator pulls a blinker behind itself. If the blinker is moved further away from the replicator, the front end advances at c/6 as usual, while the back end recedes at a slower speed, resulting in a moving wick-stretcher. (An unmoving wick-stretcher can also be created by capping the replicator with a block or eater).

Dean Hickerson found a reaction in which two replicators can push a blinker ahead of themselves, again with a speed that depends on the length of the replicator chain. When one end is capped, this can also create wick stretchers of arbitrarily slow velocity. While patterns that can use this mechanism successfully are extremely large, the basic push mechanism is shown here. It pushes a blinker up 3 and left 5 spaces. Two copies of it can push a blinker 8 spaces diagonally.

LifeWiki: Bomber, HighLife, Replicator.

Basilisk spaceships

Around January 2013, Adam Goucher, Helmut Postl, and others constructed several Basilisk spaceships (not shown) that combine the blinker push and pull mechanisms. They explicitly constructed spaceships with diagonal velocities of c/24, c/32, c/63 and c/69, although theoretically infinitely many other velocities should be possible. Adam P. Goucher wrote an article Bombers and Basilisks describing their construction. Basilisks are extremely large, so they are too large to run in Golly. The c/69 Basilisk fits in a 339358288617×339358288604 bounding box. Its head and tail are shown here.

LifeWiki: Bomber, HighLife, Replicator.

Spaceship flotillae

Most spaceship flotillae involving the basic Life c/2 orthogonal spaceships continue to work in HighLife:

  • Most basic 2-spaceship flotillae involving LWSS, MWSS, HWSS, and sidecars work, except the 3 that involve pairs of side by side MWSS and/or HWSS. The other exotic escorts (Coe ship, pushalong, X66, half X66, and 30P4­H2V0.4) fail.
  • Block and boat draggers work.
  • Schick ships and A draggers fail.
  • Basic 3-spaceship flotillae composed of two working 2-spaceship flotillae work, as do the P20 Coe ship stabilizations. Ones involving B-heptominoes fail.
  • 3-spaceship block draggers fail.
  • 3-spaceship pre-block draggers work.
  • Most Overweight spaceships escorted by LWSS, MWSS and/or HWSS work, except those that involve MWSS and/or HWSS that are side by side, or one step advanced from the back of the OWSS.
  • The Ecologist fails.
  • 4-spaceship comma draggers and 4-spaceship hive nudgers work.

3 wick-stretchers

– ⇧ ↑ ↓ ⇩ PNG RLE APG LIS SOF ZIP SRCH

Replicator¹ [3] Sawtooth 1¹ [5] Sawtooth 2¹ [5]

These involve replicators and grow diagonally at c/6. The basic replicator makes copies of itself every 12 generations. Pairs of replicators annihilate each other, and the population increases and decreases in ever-increasing steps, so it is not periodic in the same way as other patterns are.

The other two involve a replicator slowly dragging a blinker. The blinker moves much more slowly than the replicator, resulting in an ever-growing wick. The periodic behavior becomes more and more complex as the wick grows, and occurs in multiples of the base period.

LifeWiki: Blinker, Bomber, HighLife, Replicator, Sawtooth.

2 breeders

The populations increase at a super-linear rate. The average populations at time t are indicated at the end.

– ⇧ ↑ ↓ ⇩ PNG RLE APG LIS SOF ZIP SRCH

P96 Replicator gun; MR breeder¹ [23] Maximum O(12(t/96)h); Average O(4(t/96)h); P192 Rake gun; SMM breeder¹ [20] O(2(t/192)²)

The SMM breeder is a pair of stationary P192 bomber guns that produce pairs of bombers that interact to yield P96 side-shooting rakes, that fill an area with gliders. The population is quadratic with respect to time.

The MR breeder is three rakes whose output gliders produce replicators. Over time, they fill an area with replicators. Due to the XOR behavior of the replicators, and the fact that a new replicator is generated at each step of the previous replicators, these fill an area with a Sierpinski gasket whose nodes are replicators. The population is fractal with respect to time, and the h in the formula is the Hausdorff dimension of a Sierpinski Gasket, which is log2(3), or approximately 1.58496. The rakes move diagonally at c/6.

LifeWiki: Block, Glider, HighLife, Replicator.

Dead spark coils and similar objects are still-lifes in Life, but come alive in HighLife, becoming period 2 oscillators with 1×1 rotors, something that is impossible in Life.

The Cuphook and Snake pit 2 are period 3 oscillators in Life, but period 4 oscillators in HighLife, so even though the starting generations are the same in both rules, these are distinct oscillators.

Multiple lonely bees can be chained together two spaces apart in both Life and HighLife. However, HighLife also allows them to be placed one space apart.
