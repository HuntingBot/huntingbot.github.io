Introduction

All the rules described here are similar to Life, with some minor changes. Unless otherwise noted, they all are all two state (i.e. cells are dead or alive) and use a Moore neighborhood (i.e. a two-dimensional square lattice in which every cell’s neighborhoods consists of the 4 orthogonally adjacent and 4 diagonally adjacent cells).

Rules

Totalistic rules

In totalistic rules, the state of a cell in the next generation is determined solely by the number of its living neighbors, which may or may not also include itself. The particular orientation of those neighbors is not important. Two examples of such rules are studied here:

  • B2/S2 (2/2 Life): This explosive rule has both birth and survival on 2. Patterns are extremely volatile, and it is difficult to make them not expand without limit.
  • B34/S34 (3/4 Life): This explosive rule has both birth and survival on 3 or 4 neighbors. There was a fairly extensive discussion of this rule in the Lifeline newsletter in the 1970s. It is also fairly interesting, but many patterns expand infinitely, making it harder to work with.

Outer totalistic rules

Outer totalistic rules (or semi-totalistic rules) form a superset of totalistic rules. The state of a cell in the next generation is determined by both its own state, and the number of its living neighbors, excluding itself. The particular orientation of those neighbors is not important. Several examples of such rules are studied here:

  • B3/S23 (Conway’s Game of Life): This rule has birth on 3 neighbors, and survival on 2 or 3 neighbors. This is one of the most interesting, and most widely studied two-dimensional cellular automata, and the subject of most of these pages.
  • B36/S23 (HighLife): This rule is almost identical to Life, and differs only in that birth is also allowed on 6 neighbors. Its behaves very similarly to Life, but also includes a small common sawtooth replicator, and several new oscillators.
  • B36/S245; (Replicator rule): This is a rule that has a very small natural replicator. Patterns grow very differently than in Life, and do not tend to grow much, except when replicators form.
  • B3x/S23y (Life-like rules): These rules are almost identical to Life, and differ only in that birth and/or survival are also allowed on 8 neighbors. These behave very similarly to Life, but exhibit a few minor differences worth noting.
  • Multi-colored Life: This describes several enhanced multi-state versions of Life, where cells are born and die the same way as in Life, but there are multiple living states (typically indicated by different colors) that supply additional information and complexity.

Non-totalistic rules

Non-totalistic rules are a superset of outer totalistic rules. The state of a cell in the next generation is determined by both its own state, and not only the number of its living neighbors, but also their particular orientations. The most studied examples of these are isotropic (i.e. birth and survival conditions are invariant under rotation and reflection), and Golly fully supports them. One group of related rules is studied here:

  • B2n3/S23-q (LeapLife) is a rule fairly similar to Life that is distinguished by a small natural knight-ship, Lepa.
  • B2n3-q5y6c/S23-k (VesselLife), is a rule similar to LeapLife that is distinguished by several additional small natural spaceships, plus two small natural wick-stretchers that can grow ships of arbitrary length.
  • Niemiec’s Rules: This is actually several closely-related rules. While the birth conditions are outer totalistic (and, in fact, the same as Life’s), the survival conditions are not totalistic — i.e. they depend not only on the number of neighbors, but also their relative positions. Birth occurs on any 3 neighbors, while survival occurs on 2…4 neighbors that are orthogonally connected to the original cell (i.e. diagonal neighbors are allowed only if they are themselves adjacent to one or two orthogonal neighbors). There are 3 optional neighborhoods where survival may or may not be permitted. These yield 8 closely-related rule variants. Most common patterns are shared by all variants. Some characteristics of these rules are that most useful patterns are ominoes, or are composed almost entirely of ominoes; there are also 4 common spaceships, all of different velocities, several simple guns and puffers, and a common replicator, which makes some of the variants explosive.

Download Golly Rules

While most of the above rules have files that can be viewed by most Life programs, non-totalistic rules (e.g. LeapLife, VesselLife, or Niemiec’s Rules) require Golly version 2.8 and later, and two others (Color Life, and the Search Methodologies pages) can be run by Golly versions 2.5 and later, but require special rule files to first be downloaded and installed. Each of those pages includes a link to download rule files necessary to view them, or they can all be downloaded in one zip file to conveniently install them all at once. Just open the zip file, and copy all the rule files to the target directly. They can either be installed for all users (in the Rules directory directly under where the Golly application is installed), or in the current user’s personal Rule directory. (To find out where this is, run Golly, select File/Preferences…, and look at the Control tab. It is displayed right next to the Your Rules… button.
